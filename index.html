Influenza in Hong Kong?

Seasonal influenza, better known as the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by flu viruses. The World Health Organisation (WHO) estimates that such epidemic has caused 290,000 to 650,000 deaths around the world. It has rung the alarm on global healthcare.

A densely populated city like Hong Kong is no exception to this pandemic. Every January to March and July to August are the hotbeds for influenza. However, the recent unusual influenza surge in 2018 which causes ten deaths in nine days has caused grave concern among the public. Is the flu a revival of the SARs outbreak in 2003? Will the old scene of crowds of pedestrians wearing surgical mask make its comeback?

With the aim to address a health hazard as alarming as flu that plagues the Hong Kong society, and to better understand the infection pattern of the flu virus, the report explores the trend of Hong Kong’s flu, the underlying factors that causes the trend and and potential solutions that help combat the virus alongside the help from Healthcare Protection Centre’s statistics.

1. The growing trend of influenza from 2014 to 2018


Influenza-related death rates among HongKongers from 2014 to 2018

(Blue = 2014/15 winter, pink = 2017/18 winter, purple = 2015/16 winter, red = 2017 summer)



Influenza has been surging vigorously in the past four years from 2014 to 2018. In the winter of 2018, Hong Kong schools and kindergartens have been shut down amid an outbreak of a deadly flu virus that has killed more than 100 people across the region in a matter of weeks, according to figures from the Centre for Health Protection (CHP).

The last time Hong Kong officials announced school closures over a public health issue was in 2009 during the human swine flu pandemic.



The two subtypes of influenza A virus, H1N1 and H3N2, and influenza B virus, are most commonly seen. And the flu outbreaks are every year in the winter and summer, and in Hong Kong’s case, between January and March, and from July to August. But there are also unusual flu outbreaks among the years.

According to the past 4 years’ data , Virus A is the majority type among the three types, while virus C consisted only little.

Obviously, virus A had the similar developing trend with total influenza development in the past, especially highly widespread in 2017 summer, contributing to the extremely explosive influenza. It is mainly due to its easy-spread on air characteristic, especially in a high density space. Observing the data of temperature and rain in the past 4 years, we can find that June and July of 2017 is hotter and humid than the past years with more raindrop, making the virus remain for a longer time.

Even 15 years after the SARS, we still can see such a big influenza outbreak. We should always open the windows to help the air circulation. We should reinforce our awareness of preventing virus, especially by the way of wearing mask when we have seasonal infections.

As for virus B, it was unexpected highly surged in 2017/2018 winter. From January 2018 until now, virus B has spread at around 12895 cases.

There is always a peak for seasonal flu, and this winter the problem came to a head. But this one is much more different. “The predominating virus of this winter influenza season has been influenza B,” he said.



Our observation represents the overall effects of a complex interaction. Survival Virus can be transmit through the air to cause susceptibility to influenza and affect Human health. Social behaviour and ventilation can be the indirect effects, especially the high density of population in Hong Kong.

Hung Fan-ngai, professor and expert of infectious disease at the University of Hong Kong, said there have been more school outbreaks involving the dominant strain of the influenza B virus, which had not been active in the past few years.

2. The effect of vaccination

Seasonal influenza vaccination is one of the effective means to prevent seasonal influenza and its complications，according to the World Health Organisation (WHO). When the vaccine strains closely match the circulating influenza viruses, efficacy of influenza vaccine range from 70% to 90%.

Moreover, the Centre for Health Protection (CHP) noted that, most of the children killed by flu in the winter of 2017 were not vaccinated.

Although the amount of vaccines being purchased and the number of people being vaccinated are both increasing, the influenza vaccination rate is still low. Take the year 2016-2017 for an example, which is the most people were not vaccinated, accounts the participation rate of 8.37%.

3. Hong Kong's war on flu

Children and elderly people are being hospitalised with severe flu in this current flu season, figures from the Centre for Health Protection in Hong Kong show.

Hong Kong Government has launched the Government Vaccination Programme (GVP) on Nov.2017, which provide the free vaccination to eligible residents and elderly aged 65 years or above.

Public Hospital Admission Rate by Age group because of Influenza

However, less than 10% people took the vaccination which means people have not been formed the habit of vaccination, according to the Centre for Health Protection (CHP).


3. Solutions in dealing with flu?

To battle influenza in the city, Hong Kong government should facilities the health care services but also raise people’s awareness. According to what we have found, several suggestions are as follows.


3.1 Increase the influenza vaccination rate
The influenza vaccination rate has not increased obviously, around 10%-14% per year. Thus, room for improvement in influenza vaccination coverage among school children can be expanded.

To better protect the city against seasonal flu, the government should promote the vaccination programmes more aggressively, and further reduce the vaccination cost through the centralised ordering of vaccines.

Mobile vaccination vehicles should be used to make the vaccines more accessible around the city. Vaccination teams should be sent to kindergartens, primary and secondary schools to inoculate children.


3.2 Fill the shortage of manpower in medical services


Hong Kong’s flu patients are suffering, but so are doctors, nurses and public hospital staffs.Local public hospitals and emergency wards have been overflowing with patients while struggling with staff shortages.



Given the hefty surplus expected in our budget this year, the government should formulate a long-term plan in manpower development for medical services, including a holistic review of the remuneration, working conditions, demand for equipment and incentives related to medical staff at public hospitals, and subsequently devote significant amount of resources to cope with the plan.


3.3 Fill vaccine shortage


Many have complained that the city has run out of vaccines – some 460,000 doses were used up before the holiday (Lunar New Year of 2018).The government has pledged to import vaccines to meet the demand; for the time being, we are expecting another 44,000 doses.

The government should take active steps before the next peak flu season, in summer, and account for the number of vaccines arranged for that time.


3.4 Update the vaccine


This vaccine update provides immunisation practitioners with the latest developments in vaccines, vaccination policies and procedures.

Yuen Kwok-yung, professor of microbiology at the University of Hong Kong: Flu virus may mutate into influenza A, the effectiveness of the current vaccine will drop by 10 percent.

The vaccines currently available which targets at attacking the ongoing influenza B, contains 40 to 70 percent of effectiveness. However, when the flu virus mutate into influenza A, the protectiveness of the current vaccine will drop by 10 percent.


3.5 Raise people's awareness


Considering the low participation rate, the department should devote more resources to marketing its vaccination programmes.For instance, provide more opportunities for children vaccination in school and create a more flexible way for the elderly people.
